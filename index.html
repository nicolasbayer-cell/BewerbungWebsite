01Synthetic Biology

iGEM 2021 —
MOCLOMANIA

In 2021, 9 biology bachelor's students from the RPTU Kaiserslautern got together to participate in iGEM, an international scientific competition for synthetic biology. It encourages young researchers from all over the glove to develop new biotechnological approaches and connect with eachother!

Well, I was one of these nine students! And did I know what I had gotten myself into? God, no! Did I enjoy it - tremendously! We quickly came up with a very exciting research project - MOCLOMANIA: Our goal was to establish Modular Cloning in the protozoan parasite Leishmania tarentolae. Why? Because unlike bacteria, Leishmania have a human-like post-translational glycolyzation pattern, which makes them promising hosts for the recombinant production of therapeutic proteins. Furthermore, they can be farmed in big automated cultivators just like bacteria, facilitating large-scale production.

And why Modular Cloning? Because it is one of the most versatile and practical cloning systems out there! It was thought up by Weber et al. in 2011, is based on Golden Gate Cloning and utilizes different kinds of type IIS restriction enzymes which allow for the seamless construction of multi-level genetic cassettes based on individual gene components!

We created a large vector library of genetic components such as promoters, coding sequences or terminators and made them compatible for expression in Leishmania. These components work like building bricks for transcriptional units which can now be employed by others for their research or manufacturing purposes.

Our project could even be made into a publication which you can find here. You can also check out our iGEM 2021 project website where you can learn a lot more about Leishmania, Modular Cloning and the iGEM competition itself!

02Cell Biology

Bachelor's thesis —
Probing the Integrin Adhesome

After iGEM, I didn't stay in synthetic biology. Instead, a lab practical at the Nanophysiology working group by Prof. Tanja Maritzen took my interest into another direction! Their research focuses on the different molecular structures formed by mammalian cells to interact with and adhere to the extracellular matrix ECM. While some of these adhesion structures, such as focal adhesions, have already been extensively characterized by previous research and are considered canonical, a distinct class of adhesions has recently emerged as a new topic of research.

These so-called reticular adhesions RAs are considered non-canonical because they have a unique morphology and a protein pool which lacks many canonical adhesion proteins and is instead enriched in encytosis-associated proteins! The exact physiological function of these RAs, to this day, remains elusive. Lock et al. have implicated them in the stable attachment of cells to the ECM substrate, especially in the context of mitosis. The goal of Prof. Maritzen and her research crew is to identify the protein interaction network responsible for the formation, modulation and disassembly of RAs.

Like most other ECM adhesions, RAs rely on transmembrane proteins called integrins to bridge between the intracellular and extracellular environment. In fact, the specific integrin heterodimer integrin αVβ5 constitutes a hallmark of RAs. Thus, probing the protein pool surrounding these integrin dimers might help to identify other key players associated with the reticular adhesion site!

In my bachelor's thesis, I set out to investigate the protein pool associated with integrin β5 in culture conditions with enriched RA formation with the help of biotinylation-based proximity labeling! For this, I first tagged the cytoplasmic domain of integrin β5 with the engineered peroxidase APEX2. This enzyme catalyzes the oxidation of biotin phenol, generating highly reactive biotin phenoxyl radicals which covalently bind to amino acids in neighboring proteins within a specific radius. This way, all proteins in subcellular proximity to integrin β5 are labelled and can subsequently be pulled down over strepatividin beads for downstream analysis.

Due to the thesis' time limitations, only one biotinylation run could actually be analyzed via mass spectrometry. Here, many proteins expected to be enriched (since they are known to occur at RAs) in fact didn't show any enrichment. Furthermore, proteins exclusively localized to the nucleus or ER were identified as enriched, despite integrin β5 being associated with the plasma membrane. All of this implied that the biotinylation set-up wasn't yet powerful enough to produce meaningful readout beyond non-specific background biotinlytation and purification. Nonetheless, the established protocol served as a basis for other researchers to finetune and adapt the methodology to other RA constituents!

03cell biology

Master's thesis -
linking RAs and the actin cytoskeleton

When I finished my bachelor's thesis, I didn't leave the Nanophysiology lab! Instead, I started working there as a research assistant, which gave me more experience and routine with wet lab techniques and fostered my organization and time management skills. And since I liked both the research topic as well as the people in the working group, I decided to stay for my master's thesis!

In my project, we focused on the availability of the key structural protein complex filamentous actin F-actin at sites of reticular adhesion RAs. Based on inhibitor assays, Lock et al. had previously described RAs to be completely actin-independent. And indeed, RAs can persist when their connection to the actin cytoskeleton is disrupted. However, when looking at cultured wild-type epithelial cells, it soon becomes evident that most RAs are in fact actively associated with actin filaments under standards conditions!

Thus, the goal of my project was to further elucidate the relationship between F-actin and RAs. For this, I performed a ton of immunostainings and subsequent image analysis to quantify the amount of actin-associated vs. non-associated RAs in epithelial cells under different conditions.

Here, I found that cell density critically influences RA formation, but not actin association. Conversely, certain kinase inhibitors showed prominent effects on RA-actin association, hinting towards an involvement of these kinases in the recruitment or stabilization of F-actin at RAs.

Furthermore, I also looked at several different actin-regulatory proteins that are known to mediate actin tethering and polymerization in focal adhesions, but were thus far thought to be absent from RAs. While interesting tendencies emerged, I was unable to conclusively pinpoint a certain protein as critical for RA-actin association. This was not only due to several experimental issues regarding knockdown efficiency and cellular viability, but likely also stems from the partial redundancy of the many different regulatory pathways involved.

Additional live-cell imaging and FRAP revealed that actin filaments seemed to be more stable at RAs compared to canonical adhesions and that, whilst some RAs actively lose F-actin tethering throughout their lifetime, their connection to the cytoskeleton could be critical for adhesion assembly and disassembly. Conclusively, despite the interesting glimpses gained in my master's project, a lot more research has to go into the complex protein interaction network surrounding RAs in order to better understand their interconnection to the actin cytoskeleton on a molecular as well as physiological level.
